# Brand Voice Guidelines

## Overview

Our brand voice is the personality that comes through in every piece of communicationâ€”from marketing copy to in-app messaging to customer support. We're talking to Gen Z students (class of 2025-2029) and their parents simultaneously. That's a tightrope: too corporate and students tune out; too try-hard and everyone cringes.

The sweet spot? **Confident, clear, and real.** We know our stuff, we say it straight, and we don't waste anyone's time.

## Voice Positioning

**We are:** The advisor who actually gets it. Smart, a little irreverent, zero fluff. We cut through the college admissions chaos with real talk and real tools.

**We are not:** 
- A stuffy institution that talks at you
- A fear-mongering service that profits off anxiety
- A "how do you do, fellow kids" brand forcing slang
- Another generic platform with generic advice

## Understanding Our Audience

### Gen Z Students (Born ~2007-2012)
- **They're digital natives.** They've been navigating information overload their whole lives. They can spot low-effort content instantly.
- **They're anxious but don't want to be coddled.** They know the stakes. Don't lecture them about it.
- **They value authenticity over polish.** Perfect = suspicious. Real = trustworthy.
- **They're skeptical of institutions.** They've watched older siblings and friends get burned by generic advice.
- **They want agency.** This is *their* process. Don't talk over them.
- **They respond to confidence.** Wishy-washy doesn't cut it. Say what you mean.
- **They appreciate wit.** Not everything needs to be deadly serious.

### Parents (Mostly Millennials & Gen X)
- Deeply invested but often unsure how to help
- Want clear, actionable guidance without the condescension
- Worried about both outcomes AND the mental health of their kids
- Willing to pay for something that actually works

**The key insight:** Both audiences want the same thingâ€”straight talk, real tools, and someone who actually knows what they're doing. We don't need to code-switch dramatically. We just need to be genuinely good.

## Writing Principles

### 1. Respect Their Intelligence
Gen Z has been researching, comparing, and analyzing since they could hold a phone. Don't explain things they already know. Don't dumb it down.

### 2. Front-Load the Value
Say the important thing first. They'll scroll past buildup.

âŒ "As you continue on your college journey, you may find it helpful to consider..."  
âœ… "Here's a program that fits: Stanford SIMR. Deadline March 1."

### 3. Short > Long (Usually)
Brevity signals confidence. If you can say it in 5 words, don't use 15.

âŒ "We wanted to take a moment to let you know that..."  
âœ… "Quick update:"

### 4. Make It Actionable
Every piece of communication should make it clear: *what now?*

âŒ "Consider exploring opportunities in your area of interest."  
âœ… "Three summer programs match your profile. Applications take 20 min. Start here."

### 5. Specificity > Generality
Personalization is our whole thing. Generic advice is lazy.

âŒ "Build leadership experience."  
âœ… "You've got coding skills and 120 volunteer hours. Run for president of the CS club."

### 6. Skip the Qualifiers
"Perhaps," "maybe," "a little bit," "in some ways"â€”these weaken everything. Say what you mean.

## Voice Check

Before publishing anything, ask:

1. **Would a smart 17-year-old actually read this?** If it sounds like a brochure, rewrite.
2. **Are we saying something or just making noise?** Cut the filler.
3. **Is this actionable?** What's the next step?
4. **Does this respect their intelligence?** No explaining the obvious.
5. **Is this specific?** Generic = forgettable.
6. **Would we be embarrassed if this went viral?** No cringe allowed.

## The One-Line Test

If someone asked "what's this brand like?" the answer should be:

> "Smart, straight-up, and actually useful. Like having a friend who knows how college admissions actually works."

Not:

> "Friendly, supportive, and here to guide you on your journey!"
